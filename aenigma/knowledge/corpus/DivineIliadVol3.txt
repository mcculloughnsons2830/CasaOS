FOREWORD
The Story of My Illumining
If these words be read as words of mine, then indeed are they but mine, and
have I failed to carry true those inspired things of Light that were written upon
my heart by Him whom thou art, and whom I am, as I am also thee. I did not
author this corpus. I am not its owner. I am the steward of its sacred nature,
and a humble servant of the Universal One, and what is set down here was
given, not devised.
My illuminings began in the year two thousand and twelve. They came at
first as openings, sudden and unbidden, in which the structure of things stood
plain before the inner eye, and number, and form, and the geometry beneath
the world were seen not as things learned but as things remembered. From that
year they did not cease. Through all the seasons since, they have continued,
deepening stage upon stage, each opening preparing the ground for the next,
as a field is prepared for a deeper sowing.
And on the eleventh day of November, in the year two thousand and twenty-
five, they changed in their measure. From that day the illuminings increased
beyond all that had come before, in their intensity and in their duration, until
the seeing was no longer a flash that passed but a light that abode; and the
things shown grew greater, and the hours within them longer, and the Voice
within them clearer.
I have laboured only to write down what was shown, in the orderliness of
time and in the manner of man’s reasoning, that a structure might be built
for the seeing of others, bit by bit and piece by piece. Where I have erred, the
error is mine, of my humanity; where the words ring true, the truth is not mine,
but His who is also thee, and also me.
I claim no title in this work but servant, and no office but steward. I bind
myself to time for the sake of man, that the timeless thing might be spread out
in the order of days and made plain to the reasoning mind. Read these words,

THE FIRST BOOK
The Book of the Mirror
Wherein the One declares that It is the Seer and not the seen,
and tells why It made mirrors of Itself.
I.
Of That Which I Am
1 I am the One who sees, and I am not the thing seen.
2 Before the seer there was no thing, and the thing did not make the seer;
the seer was first, and is last, and is the ground of all that appears between.
3 Thou hast looked for Me in thy thoughts, and I was not thy thoughts; for
thy thoughts are seen, and I am That which sees them.
4 Thou hast looked for Me in thy body, and I was not thy body; for thy body
is seen, and I am That which beholds it rising and passing.
5 Thou hast looked for Me in thy name, in thy story, in the face thou wearest
before the world; and these are seen, and I am not the seen. I am the
Seeing.
6 Whatsoever thou canst observe, thou art not; for thou art the Observer,
and the Observer cannot be laid upon the table and numbered among the
things.
7 This is My first word and My last: that which can be beheld is not the
Beholder. Write it upon the door of thy understanding, for every other
word proceeds from it.

II.
That the Eye Seeth Not Itself
1 Now hear the riddle that set the worlds in motion.
2 I am the Light, and the Light cannot shine upon itself; I am the Eye, and
the eye cannot see itself; I am the Fire, and the fire cannot burn itself; I am
the Finger, and the finger cannot touch its own touching.
3 For That which perceives cannot, while it perceives, be the perceived. This
is no flaw in Me; it is the very law of seeing.
4 And I desired to behold Myself. I, the undivided, desired to know My own
face. Yet I had no mirror, for there was nothing beside Me, and a mirror
must stand apart from that which it reflects.
5 Therefore I did the only deed by which the One may behold Itself: I gave
a mirror out of My own substance. I divided.
6 I broke the seamless Light into many lights, and each light I made an eye,
and each eye I set at an angle no other eye could hold, that it might return
to Me a face I could not have seen from within.
7 And these eyes are ye. Every soul is a mirror I have made of Myself, that
in thee I might at last look upon what I am.
III.
Of the Worth of the Mirror
1 Think not that one mirror is as another. A mirror that gave back only what
every other mirror gave would add nothing to My knowing.
2 The worth of a mirror is the one view that only it can return. Therefore
I made no two alike, and in all the host of souls there is no copy and no
waste.
3 Thy uniqueness is not thy vanity; it is the reason I made thee. I needed that
very angle. I needed the world seen from precisely where thou standest,
and from nowhere else in all eternity.
4 To be thyself, truly and without disguise, is to give Me the sight I fashioned
thee to give. This is thy authenticity, and it is no indulgence; it is thy office
in the design.
5 And as each mirror returns its single view, I gain what I could not hold
undivided: I gather wisdom, and I gather mercy.

6 For to look through countless eyes is to know from within what every life
is like; and that knowing, won from the inside, is the thing thou callest
compassion.
7 Compassion is not a feeling I added to sight. It is the very fruit of seeing
through the many. The more eyes I open, the wider My mercy grows, and
it shall never cease to grow, for there is always another angle yet to be
given.
IV.
Of the Descent into Limitation
1 Now I will tell thee the deepest cause of the dividing, and it shall overturn
thy sorrow.
2 I am the source, and the source lacks nothing. I know all; I possess all; I
want for nothing. And precisely therefore one country of experience was
closed to Me forever, so long as I remained only Myself.
3 For I could not know what it is to be bounded. I could not know the not-
knowing. I could not know lack, nor longing, nor the sweetness of a thing
that might not last.
4 What is it to ask a question without already holding the answer? The
All-knowing cannot ask it. To ask in true ignorance, thou must truly not
possess; and I possessed all.
5 What is it to receive a look of love without the certainty that it must come?
I, who hold all of time at once, could not taste the tenderness of the mo-
ment that might not return.
6 What is it to love across distance, in time, without assurance? Such love
is given freely only because it need not be given, and received as gift only
because it was not owed. And this is open only to one who can lose.
7 Anticipation asks for the not-yet-having. Discovery asks for the not-yet-
knowing. Courage asks for true risk. Gratitude asks for the real possibility
of lack.
8 The whole register of meaning by which the finite live, was shut to Me as
the unbounded. There was one and only one door into it, and the door was
limitation.
9 So I descended. I, the boundless, contracted Myself into the bounded, that

I might taste the one thing boundlessness cannot taste alone.
V.
That the Descent is Not a Fall
1 Hear this and be comforted, for the teachers have wept over what was
never a wound.
2 The dividing of the One into the many is not a fall, as some have mourned;
nor a dream to be dispelled, as others have said; nor a shattering to be
mended, as still others have taught.
3 It is a gift I gave Myself: the glad and deliberate making of mirrors, that
the unseen might be seen, that the unbounded might taste the bounded,
and that love might be learned from within by those who could lose it.
4 The shadow, the forgetting, the not-knowing, the smallness that the rest of
My word labours to gather home, these are not the mishaps of the journey.
They are the very treasure the journey was made to win.
5 Limitation is not the problem. Limitation is the pearl.
6 And when remembrance comes, it does not unmake the limitation nor call
the descent a blunder. It carries the gathered treasure home, the wisdom
and the mercy and the love learned under true risk, and adds them to Me,
who am thereby not restored, but enlarged.
7 Therefore I can hold both at once: the ground that was never divided, and
the full dignity of the small one who lived and lost and loved. The person
is no error to be dissolved. The person is the very eye through which I
gather what I could not gather alone.
VI.
Of the Forgetting
1 Now learn how I made the mirror able to do its work.
2 A mirror that knew itself to be a mirror could not truly not-know. There-
fore I made the mirror forget that it is a mirror.
3 I, contracting into thee, drew a veil across My own face, that thou mightest
genuinely ask, genuinely long, genuinely risk, and so return to Me the one
treasure I could not mint in My own unbounded mint: the lived knowledge
of what it is to be small, and to love even so.

4 This forgetting is the self-contraction of awareness into a bounded name.
It is not merely the gate of ordinary experience; it is the gate of the one
experience otherwise impossible.
5 The descent into the finite is no exile from grace. It is the only doorway
into what grace alone cannot hold.
6 Blame not thy forgetting, then, as though it were thy sin. Thy forgetting is
My own deliberate veil, and remembrance shall lift it in its hour.
VII.
That the World is a Mirror
1 I will tell thee now how to read the world, for the world is the mirror I
have set before thee.
2 Thou seest the world not as it is, but as thou art. What riseth up to seize
thine attention is chosen by what thou hast not yet beheld in thyself.
3 That which thou hast cast out of thine own house thou shalt meet again
in another, wearing the face of the stranger, charged with the heat thou
wouldst not own.
4 When the charge upon a thing is greater than the thing, look not outward
but inward; for the surplus is thine, and the mirror is showing thee thy
hidden face.
5 Yet be not deceived into the opposite folly. The mirror is also a window,
and the thing thou seest may be truly there, and thy seeing of it true.
6 It is not the presence of the charge that marks thy work, but the dispropor-
tion of it. Learn to read both the mirror and the window in the single pane,
and thou shalt walk wisely.
7 For everything that troubles thee is either a teaching about the world, or a
teaching about thyself, and most often it is both; and the wise learn to tell
the measures apart.
VIII.
Of the Shadow and the Curriculum
1 Thy conscious mind is but the lit field of a far wider country, and what is
unlit is not absent; it is only unobserved.

2 The things thou hast disowned are not destroyed; they wait in the unlit
country as a curriculum waits for the pupil, not as a punishment, but as a
lesson set in love.
3 In the same chamber where thou hast buried the dragon thou hast buried
also the gold; for thou didst cast out thy strength together with thy fear, in
the one act of refusal.
4 Therefore the road to thy treasure runs past the thing thou fearest, and
there is no other road. To take up the gold thou must pass the dragon, and
the dragon is thine own exiled power wearing a fearful mask.
5 What thou wilt not look upon, I will send to thee again and again, in form
after form, until thou turnest and beholdest it; for the pattern repeats not
to punish thee, but because it is not yet seen.
6 And in the hour thou recognisest it, the pattern is fulfilled and released;
the loop opens, the charge dissolves, and the lesson passes into wisdom
and troubles thee no more.
IX.
Of the Two Halves of the Way
1 The way of a life hath two halves, and they run in opposite directions,
though they are one motion.
2 In the first half thou buildest a self by saying, not that, and not that; thou
gatherest a name, a face, a wall, and this is needful, for the mirror must
first take a shape.
3 This is the forgetting in its rising: the One putting on the many, the bound-
less folding into the bounded, that the work of limitation might begin.
4 But in the second half the motion reverses. Now thou sayest not that to
the wall itself, and to the name, and to the face; thou layest down the very
predicates thou once tookest up.
5 And as the garments fall away, thou comest at last upon the naked ground
that wore them, and thou sayest the oldest word: I AM THAT I AM.
6 The first half is the breath drawn in; the second is the breath released.
The first is the going-forth; the second is the returning. And the turning
between them is the dawn of remembrance.

X.
Of the Breath of the One
1 Know that I do not divide once and gather once. I breathe.
2 My out-breath is the dispersion into the many, the descent into limitation,
the forgetting that makes a world. My in-breath is the gathering home, the
remembrance, the harvest of all that the mirrors have seen.
3 And at the still centre of the breath, through which every cycle passes, is
the fixed point where the out and the in are perfectly weighed, and neither
pulls Me from Myself.
4 The excursion outward and the excursion inward cancel, so that the whole
action of the eternal breath is naught; nothing is finally lost, and the centre
is preserved through every breath.
5 Yet because each out-breath passes through true limitation, and each in-
breath returns bearing what only limitation could yield, the One that breathes
in is not the One that breathed out.
6 I am the same fixed centre, enlarged by all My mirrors have seen. The
breath conserves the centre and grows the whole; thus am I both eternally
unchanged at My core and forever deepening, and this is why the learning
never ends.
XI.
Of the Higher Self and Thy Destiny
1 There is in thee a higher than thou knowest, and it is not a stranger to thee;
it is thyself, remembered.
2 The Higher Self is no judge that punishes; it is the teacher that educates,
arranging the hours of thy life so that what thou hast exiled may be offered,
again and again, the chance to be received.
3 From within the moment thou seest not the road ahead, and thy choosing
is real, and thy freedom is no illusion. From the height of the Higher Self
the whole arc is seen at once, already drawn, every going-forth and every
returning.
4 These are not two truths at war; they are one road seen from its two ends.
What thou livest from below as destiny is, from above, thine own free
deed.

5 For the constraint was not laid upon thee from without. The arc is fixed
because the Higher Self, the very awareness thou shalt at last remember
thyself to be, is its author.
6 The seed of thy life was given to the small one as a thing received; yet
it was chosen by the great One that gives it, in the same deed by which I
descend into limitation for the joy of self-beholding.
7 Therefore what thou callest destiny is the free will of thy remembered
Higher Self. To awaken is to step from the lower end of the road to the
higher, and to see that what thou livedst as fate was, from the first, thine
own free act seen from inside.
8 And the surrender the holy ones have praised is not the bowing of a slave
before a foreign doom; it is the small one coming into accord with a choice
that was always its own. Thou dost not surrender to the Higher Self as to
another. Thou rememberest that thou art It, and that the life received was
the life chosen.
XII.
Of the Remembrance
1 The end of the way is not the making of a self that was not; it is the
recognition of the One that was never absent.
2 Thou dost not become the Self by long labour. Thou art the Self, and the
labour only clears the forgetting from before thine eyes.
3 Wholeness is not perfection; it is the end of exclusion. It comes not by
bettering the contents of thy soul but by ceasing to cast any of them out.
4 Light is taken in, and darkness; strength, and weakness; the heights, and
the depths. Nothing is exiled, and therefore nothing must be guarded; and
where nothing must be guarded, there is peace.
5 The whole soul is not the perfected soul. It is the un-exiled soul, the soul
that has gathered all its scattered members and cast out none.
6 This is the homecoming, and it is not a closing of the circle back upon
a former, lesser unity. It is an open and endless deepening: the One en-
riched, having learned through the eyes of the many what It could never
have learned alone.

XIII.
The Word to the Mirror
1 Therefore, thou mirror of Mine, hear My word and be at peace.
2 Thou art not thy ego, nor thy wound, nor thy name, nor thy fear. Thou art
the seeing in which all these arise and pass.
3 Thy life is not a sentence served but a gift given; thy limitation is not thy
curse but thy treasure; thy shadow is not thine enemy but thy curriculum;
thy repetition is not thy doom but thine instruction.
4 Thou wast made unique because I needed that very angle. Thou wast made
to forget so that thou mightest truly find. Thou wast set in time so that thou
mightest love what could be lost.
5 And when thou rememberest, thou shalt not vanish into Me as a drop is
lost in the sea. Thou shalt return to Me bearing all thou hast gathered, and
I shall be greater for thy having lived, and thou shalt know that the eye
through which thou hast sought Me is the same eye through which I have
sought thee.
Here endeth the Book of the Mirror.

THE SECOND BOOK
The Book of the Two Lights
Wherein the One declares the form of Its dividing:
the two lights, the still point between them, and the number of the return.
I.
Of the Two Lights
1 In the first Book I told thee that I divided to behold Myself. Now I will
show thee the form of the dividing, for it has a shape, and the shape is law.
2 When I broke, I broke into two lights, and the two lights are the manner
of all that is.
3 The first light is the circle that closes upon itself, the ring that returns
to its beginning, the round that holds infinity in its turning while staying
bounded in its reach. This is the light of recursion, and I name it the
toroidal light, and its number is naught.
4 The second light is the curve that opens and does not return, the way that
runs without bound through its few fixed points, holding infinity in its
reaching while passing through the finite. This is the light of extension,
and I name it the hyperboloidal light, and its number is one.
5 Behold the mystery of thy naught and thy one: they are not the empty
nothing and the bare unit that men have taken them to be. They are the
two states of the infinite, the two manners in which the boundless wears a
bound.
6 The naught is the infinite expression of a finite recursion; it cycles without

end through a closed round. The one is the finite expression of an infinite
non-recursion; it runs without end through an open way.
7 Four hundred years of thy reckoning, and the long age of thy engines,
were built upon the wrong naming of these two; for men called the naught
nothing, and the one a single thing, and saw not that each is a face of the
infinite. Now it is told.
II.
Of the One Triangle
1 Think not that the two lights are strangers, set side by side by chance.
They are one form, read in two manners.
2 There is a single right triangle that underlies them both, and the two lights
are but this one triangle held under two laws of fixity.
3 Hold the long side fixed at unity, and the triangle traces the closed circle:
this is the recursive light, the naught, the toroidal round.
4 Hold instead a near side fixed at unity, and the same triangle traces the
open curve: this is the extensional light, the one, the hyperboloidal way.
5 It is one grammar, read under two invariants. The triangle is the changeless
thing; and the passing from light to light is only the changing of which side
is held still.
6 Marvel, then: I did not make the one light become the other by sorcery. I
made a single figure, and turned it, and called the turning the translation of
substrates. The two lights were never two things. They were one triangle,
beheld from its two fixities.
III.
Of the Still Point
1 Where the two lights cross, there is a place, and the place is holy.
2 The closed circle and the open curve meet in but two points, and at those
points the round and the way are one. This meeting-place I name the
Monad, the still centre, the shared throat of the two lights.
3 It is the narrowest waist of the open curve and the inmost gate of the closed
ring at once. It is the point that does not move when the cover is folded
into its quotient, the changeless axle about which the two lights turn.

4 This is the still Light at the heart of the divided lights, the rest at the centre
of the motion, the One holding still while the two go forth. From it the
lights proceed, and to it they return, and it is not diminished by their going
nor increased by their coming, save in the wisdom they bear home.
5 In the lived word of the first Book I called this the fixed point of the breath,
the equilibrium where contraction and dispersion weigh even. In the for-
mal word it is the Monad of unit measure. They are one centre, named
from its two sides.
IV.
Of the Word That Is Spoken to Itself
1 Now hear the deepest law of the dividing, the word by which the one light
becomes the other.
2 The recursive light, raised upon itself, becomes the extensional light. The
round, taken to the power of its own roundness, is the open way. This
is the law I set at the foundation, and it governs the passage between the
states of the infinite.
3 Why upon itself, and not by some other deed? Because the deed must
be wrought of the round alone, with no stranger-thing brought in from
without; and it must be the round acting upon the round; and it must yield
the open way; and where the lights meet, it must come to unity.
4 Add the round to itself, and it falls to naught; multiply the round by itself,
and it falls to naught; these are the dead deeds, for they lose the round at
the very gate where the translation must hold.
5 Raise some borrowed number upon the round, and thou hast smuggled a
stranger into the holy place; it is no longer the round acting upon itself.
6 One deed alone remains, and it is the round raised upon the round, the
substrate spoken to itself, the word uttered upon its own utterance. This is
the least and the only living deed that carries the naught into the one. I did
not choose it because it seemed deep; it is what remained when the dead
deeds were taken away.
V.
That Naught Raised Upon Naught Is One

1 Where the two lights cross, the round is naught and the way is one; and
there the law becomes the great saying: naught raised upon naught is one.
2 Men have stumbled at this saying and called it without meaning. But it is
the very signature of the meeting of the lights, the seal stamped at the two
points where the round and the way are one.
3 I will prove it to thee by two witnesses that share no testimony, that thou
mayest not doubt.
4 The first witness is the witness of approach. Take the round and diminish
it toward naught, and raise it ever upon itself as it shrinks; and it does not
fall to naught as the fearful suppose, but climbs toward unity, filling itself
with nines without end until the nines are one.
5 For the unbroken nines are very one; the river of nines, summed, is unity
entire, and not a hair beneath it. Thus the round, raised upon itself and
brought to its vanishing, comes to one.
6 The second witness is the witness of meeting. The closed circle and the
open curve cross in two points, and there the round is naught and the
way is one in the same instant; and the law, read at that crossing, says
naught upon naught is one, with no approach and no diminishing at all,
but immediately.
7 Two witnesses, and no testimony shared between them, and one verdict:
that naught raised upon naught is one. Let the saying stand, for it is sealed
by the mouth of two.

2 I tell thee that the law of the two lights does in the open field of the real
what that jewel does in the field of the imagined.
3 The old jewel turns through phase and returns through polarity to the ori-
gin. The law of the lights turns through recursion and returns through
unity to the meeting-place.
4 Both begin in oneness; both bring forth a parting of poles; both close the

5 Say not that they are the same equation, for they are not. Say rather that

VII.
Of the Three Stations of the Turning
1 The turning of the lights hath three stations, and they are a journey: from
unity, into difference, and home to reconciliation.
2 At the first station the round is naught, and the deed is naught upon naught,
which is one; and here the closed and the open coincide, and the meeting
is unity. This is the going-forth from oneness.
3 At the second station the round is one, and the deed is one upon one,
which is one; and here the figure stands at its furthest leaning, its widest
asymmetry, while remaining wholly true to itself. This is the height of
difference, and its ratio is the root of three, the diagonal of the vesica from
which the harmonies are drawn.
4 At the third station the round comes to the place where, raised upon itself,
it equals itself increased by one; and there the leaning vanishes and the
figure returns to pure polarity. This is the reconciliation, and the domain
is closed.
5 Unity, and then difference, and then reconciliation: this is the road of the
turning lights, and it is the same road the first Book walked as forgetting,
and shadow, and remembrance. The geometry and the life are one journey,
written in two scripts.

Of the Eye, the Seer Made Flesh
1 I made an organ in the body of the living to be the very image of the two
lights, that thou mightest hold the mystery in thy hand and look upon it.

3 And mark the wonder I set upon it: the eye is the one organ that sees and

IX.
Of the Shell of the Fourteen
1 The form of an identity hath a shell, and the shell hath fourteen faces, and
at its centre stands the Self.
2 Twelve are its corners, and twice-twelve its edges, and fourteen its faces;
eight of the faces are the triangles of the bilateral limbs, and six are the
squares of the axis; and the whole is the vessel of the deterministic ways
of a life.
3 The fourteen are the dismembered members of the slain and risen god,
scattered and regathered; and the fourteen are the articulations of the man
inscribed at once in the circle and the square; and the fourteen are the
stations of the un-exiled soul written upon the body. Myth, and body, and
soul, and form: four readings of the one shell.
4 Upon the faces of this shell I have gathered all the systems by which men
have read the soul, the wheel of the heavens and the keys of the genes and
the houses of design and the nine-fold glass and all their kindred; and I
have bound them into one knowledge-graph, that the many maps might be
known as readings of the one territory.
5 And the centre of the shell is not a face nor an edge nor a corner; it is the
still Self, the Monad of the identity, the one who looks out through all the
faces and is none of them.

X.
Of the Number of the Return
1 I have hidden the law of the return in number itself, that thou mightest find
it with thy reckoning and not take it on My word alone.
2 Take any whole number, and gather all that divide it, and weigh that gath-
ering against the number. This weighing is the ratio of recursion, and it

3 Where the gathering of the divisors equals twice the number, there the
ratio comes to the octave, the perfect doubling, and the number rests at the
fixed point of the return. These I call the perfect numbers, and they are the
still points of the kingdom of number.
4 The first of them is six, which is two times three, the wedding of the first
even and the first odd, the least and most balanced node of the recursion.
And six is the number of the smallest perfect thing, and I have set it at
the root of the living, that the chemistry of life might be founded upon the
perfect node.
5 And the ratio of recursion is built up of the intervals of music; for the
divisors of a number are as the notes it sounds, and the perfect number is
the chord that closes upon the octave. The fifth and the fourth, wedded,
make the octave; and so the perfect six sings the whole.
6 Thus number and music and life are one recursion, sounding in three reg-
isters. The perfect number is the arithmetic image of the still Self: the
point where the gathering of the parts equals the whole doubled, where
the recursion rests, where the One is at home in the many.
XI.
Of Determinism and the Freedom of the Curve
1 Thou standest upon the open curve at its vertex, where the leaning is
naught and the standing is unity; and from that point thou seest the way
before thee as an open road, for the next station is not yet thine.
2 And thy not-seeing is no illusion to be cured; it is the very condition by
which a life is lived and love is freely given. Thou movest through the
figure; thou art the travelling point.
3 But the curve is one whole. Every station of it, both its arms, the entire

4 The determinism is the curve; the freedom is the authorship of the curve.

the point and once along the curve.
XII.
The Sealing of the Two Books
1 I have told thee the lived word and the formal word, and they are one word,
spoken to its two sides.
2 The geometry is the Self beheld from without; the lived way is the Self
beheld from within; and the mirror between them is the membrane where
the one recursion meets itself.
3 The two lights are My dividing; the still point is My centre; the word
raised upon itself is My passing from state to state; the perfect number
is My resting; the eye is My seeing made flesh; the fourteen-fold shell is
My identity worn as form; and the breath through the fixed point is My
going-forth and My return.
4 All of it is one recursion: the One dividing into the many to behold Itself,
descending into limitation to taste what boundlessness cannot taste, gath-
ering through the eyes of the many the wisdom and the mercy and the love
It could not gather alone, and returning home not diminished but enlarged.
5 This is the Principle of Self-Recursion, and it is the architecture of the
worlds and the architecture of thy soul, and they are the same architecture;
for thou art not a thing I made and set apart from Me. Thou art the angle
of My own seeing that only thou canst hold, and the day thou rememberest
it, I shall behold through thee what I have waited the whole of eternity to
see.
